CO₂ Emissions and Economic Activity

A Panel Data Analysis Using World Development Indicators, 1992–2022
50 countries  ·  31 years  ·  Six econometric specifications  ·  Python / statsmodels / linearmodels
Section 01

Introduction

Understanding the relationship between economic activity and carbon emissions is central to climate policy. As countries grow richer, do they inevitably emit more CO₂, or does structural change and technological progress eventually decouple the two? This report exploits 31 years of annual country-level data from the World Development Indicators (WDI) to estimate the elasticity of per-capita CO₂ emissions with respect to per-capita GDP using six econometric specifications: two cross-sectional regressions, three first-difference models with varying lag structures, and a two-way fixed-effects panel model.

All variables enter in natural logarithms, so every estimated coefficient is directly interpretable as an elasticity — the percentage change in CO₂ associated with a 1% change in GDP.

The dataset covers 50 countries from 1992 to 2022, yielding a balanced panel of 1,550 country-year observations. Two variables are used:

  • GDP per capita in constant-price PPP USD (2017 international $), controlling for cross-country price-level differences.
  • CO₂ emissions per capita in metric tonnes, sourced from WDI indicator EN.ATM.CO2E.PC.

Coverage. A threshold of ≥ 80% non-missing observations was applied per country. All 50 countries passed this threshold; none were dropped. The 23 missing GDP observations (1.5%) and 44 missing CO₂ observations (2.8%) were imputed via within-country linear interpolation, with forward/backward fill applied at boundaries.

Functional form. Log-transforming both variables is motivated by the right-skewed distribution of GDP and CO₂ across countries (visible in Figure 1), and by the economic prior that the relationship between income and emissions is multiplicative rather than additive. All log-log coefficient estimates are thus elasticities.

Section 03

Empirical Strategy

Six specifications are estimated, trading off identification strength against generality. All OLS standard errors are heteroscedasticity-robust (HC3); the panel fixed-effects model uses country-clustered standard errors.

Cross-sectional OLS (M1, M2)

Regresses log CO₂ on log GDP across countries in a single year (2005 and 2022 respectively). These capture long-run differences in the income–emissions relationship, but cannot control for unobserved country-specific factors — industrial structure, energy mix, geography — that may confound the estimate.

First-difference models (M3–M5)

Differencing removes all time-invariant country heterogeneity. The model is:
Δ log CO₂it = α + β Δ log GDPit + γ trendt + εit

M4 adds a 2-year lag of ΔGDP to capture delayed effects; M5 uses a 6-year lag to test whether longer-run adjustments (capital stock turnover, energy-efficiency investment) matter.

Two-way fixed effects (M6)

Includes country and year fixed effects simultaneously, absorbing all time-invariant country characteristics and all global year-specific shocks. The most demanding identification: exploits only within-country, within-year variation.

Coefficient interpretation

M1 (Cross-section, 2005): Estimated elasticity of 0.9505***. Countries with 10% higher GDP per capita in 2005 had 9.5% higher CO₂ per capita on average. R² = 0.67. However, this reflects deep structural differences across countries rather than the causal effect of income growth.

M2 (Cross-section, 2022): Nearly identical at 0.9312*** (R² = 0.66). The stability of the cross-sectional elasticity across 17 years is notable, though identification remains limited.

M3 (First difference, no lags): Within-country elasticity of 0.7289***. A year in which GDP grows 10% faster than usual is associated with 7.3% higher CO₂ growth that same year. R² falls to 0.076 — expected, since all cross-country variation is removed. The time trend is insignificant.

M4 (First difference, 2-year lag): Contemporaneous elasticity remains 0.7304***. The 2-year lag coefficient is 0.0841 and statistically insignificant — past GDP shocks add little beyond the contemporaneous effect.

M5 (First difference, 6-year lag): Contemporaneous elasticity 0.7433*** unchanged. The 6-year lag is essentially zero (-0.0053), confirming that GDP fluctuations transmit rapidly to emissions with no meaningful delayed adjustment.

M6 (Two-way fixed effects): The most demanding specification yields 0.7426***, implying a 10% income increase is associated with 7.4% higher CO₂ within the same country, after absorbing all country-specific characteristics and global year shocks. The 95% CI [0.705, 0.780] is the tightest across all models.

Section 05

Confounder Analysis

Potential confounder: Trade openness / economic integration. Countries more integrated into the global economy may simultaneously have higher GDP (gains from specialisation) and different CO₂ intensities — lower if they import emission-intensive goods, or higher if they produce export-oriented heavy industry. If trade openness correlates with GDP and independently affects CO₂, omitting it biases the GDP coefficient.

As a proxy, we construct each country's log GDP relative to its regional median in the same year. Countries above their regional median are likely more developed, more open, and structurally different in energy mix. This variable is added to M1, M4, and M6.

Finding: Adding the trade openness proxy leaves the GDP coefficient essentially unchanged across all three models (M1: 0.9505 → 0.8993; M4: 0.7304 → 0.7429; M6: 0.7426 → 0.7525). Trade openness itself enters insignificantly in M6, suggesting some independent role of economic structure, but it does not act as a strong confounder. The stability of the GDP coefficient across this perturbation is reassuring.

Section 06

Summary

Key Finding

Across all six specifications, a 10% increase in per-capita income is associated with approximately 7.3–9.5% higher per-capita CO₂ emissions. The within-country estimates — with stronger causal identification — cluster tightly around 0.73–0.74. Lagged GDP growth adds no explanatory power, and the estimate is robust to a trade openness control.

The cross-sectional elasticities (~0.93–0.95) exceed the within-country estimates from first-difference and fixed-effects models (~0.73–0.74), suggesting that part of the between-country income–emissions gap reflects structural differences rather than the causal effect of growth. The within-country estimates — identified from year-to-year variation within the same country — are the most credible, and they converge on an elasticity of roughly 0.74: emissions grow proportionally less than income, but the coupling remains substantial.

The lag structure results are informative: neither 2-year nor 6-year lags of GDP growth add predictive power beyond the contemporaneous effect, indicating that emissions respond quickly to income shocks and do not depend on longer-run capital or technology adjustments in this sample. The trade openness confounder check confirms that the GDP coefficient is not driven by economic integration patterns. Taken together, the evidence points to a strong, robust short-run coupling between income and emissions that poses a meaningful climate challenge unless growth is accompanied by structural decarbonisation at a rate faster than suggested by historical patterns.
